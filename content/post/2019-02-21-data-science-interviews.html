1 Summary

In this post I share how I spent my time during my data scientist job search. I then use this information to point out things I would have done differently and things I‚Äôm glad I did. The content of this blogpost was first presented at R-Ladies London on 2019-02-21 and the presentation is here:

To summarise:

Would have done differently:

  • Establish healthy boundaries with recruiters
  • Focus on the basics for technical tests
  • Schedule interviews at favourable times

3 Here‚Äôs how I spent my time

I track time spent working to constantly update my learning loop and efficiently allocate time. I tried to extend this to my job search by tracking as many activities as possible. Here is the key for the tasks I show in the plot along with a short commentary.

3.1 Adapting CVs

On the 5th January 2019 I met up with my mentor (more on this later), we went through my CV and my LinkedIn profile in great detail. After, I dedicated a few hours to putting what she suggested into practice. This task had a great time investment.

3.2 Face-to-face interviews

This is one of my to-improve points. I scheduled four interviews in one week just two days after I moved countries with all my belongings. DO NOT DO THIS. This was not necessary. I could have spaced out interviews more into the future and with more days in between each one. This definitely had a negative impact on my performance on interviews, especially those scheduled later in the week.

3.3 Filling out forms

This was a surprise. I thought I would have had to fill out more job applications than I did. The time spent here was minimal because I ended up working mostly with recruiters. I wish I would have tracked it but most of my applications were through LinkedIn. I had the intention to also fill in my details on other job search platforms like Indeed, etc. but this ended up not being necessary.

3.4 Interview prep

This includes researching companies, preparing for technical tests, doing technical tests, notice the peak on 17th January? That was me prettifying plots as part of a technical test üíÖ

During my interview week I split the day between being at interviews and then cramming for the next. This was not fun and most definitely led to sub-optimal results.

3.5 Job admin

This is basically everything related to sending e-mails to arrange phone calls, or interviews, and towards the end, going over the final job offer, signing, sealing it and delivering it to my new employer üéµ!

3.6 Phone calls

This is another point to improve. At the beginning I was taking way too many unexpected phone calls from recruiters. This was an inefficient use of our time. After a bit I tried to push back by asking to send job specs first and then scheduling calls. More on this later. Phone calls also included technical interviews but these were actually only about two hours of the total phone call time.

3.7 Writing cover letters

Again, surprisingly low. Only wrote one cover letter as part of an application and didn‚Äôt hear back from that company at all. The data scientist job search works in mysterious ways.

4 What would I have done differently?

Here‚Äôs the fun bit where I tell you how I fell down and scratched my knee so you don‚Äôt have to.

4.1 Establish healthy boundaries with recruiters

This was my fast time working with recruiters. I wasn‚Äôt sure about how much time and attention I should allocate to working with them and/or how to do it. At the beginning I always picked up the phone. Most of the conversations didn‚Äôt add much value and were more of an interruption and distraction. I wasted a lot of time and headspace taking random recruiter calls that could have been invested elsewhere.

What would I do now?

After a week of this, I set this workflow for myself. My first point of contact is either LinkedIn or my e-mail. If someone were to reach out with a fantastic opportunity that seems especially well suited for me, I would say, ‚Äúsounds great, please send over the job spec first‚Äù. I would then sit down and read the job spec. After, I would Google the company, find the employees on LinkedIn and pause. Then I would reflect on whether I want to go forward or not. 4.2 Focus on the basics for technical test prep

There are statistics and programming basics that every data scientist should know. You would think that because they are basics you would never forget them. Not me. During the last few months I had focused on re-writing manuscripts based on analysis done in the summer. I enjoyed the Christmas/New Year break and was focused on moving countries. I hadn‚Äôt touched some bread and butter activities in a few months nor did I think about refreshing basic data wrangling or stats 101. Instead I was out to impress and googled advanced data science interview questions. This strategy was very flawed. I failed some technical questions that were not hard, I was just out of practice. When I think about it, I was just asked one neural network question, the rest was basics.

What would I do now?

I would focus less on specific algorithm details and just throughly freshen up on stats 101, data cleaning and wrangling basics by heart including syntax in both R and SQL.

4.3 Schedule interviews at favourable times

In retrospective I realise I was somewhat nervous about having any time gaps in my CV. This led me to cram moving countries and going to job interviews. I was so excited about the search that I allowed interviews to be scheduled one after another just a few days after moving house. This wasn‚Äôt necessary and of course had a negative impact on my job search. I couldn‚Äôt devote my full attention to interview prep.

What would I do now?

I don‚Äôt think leaving a one month time gap would have had any negative consequences. Instead it would have given me enough time to settle in to start ramping up the interviewing process. I would also try to schedule interviews with at least one day in between and in the morning, if possible.

4.4 Rehearse not knowing, or being wrong, and feeling embarrassed/nervous/uncomfortable and STILL speaking with confidence

I have a new found respect for politicians now. When they announce a policy or a plan at a press conference, inevitably they will be asked something they do not know. They might even be corrected on something they just said. The ability to keep calm or appear calm is something they have practiced over and over. I had no idea how important this skill is to practice until I went through this round of interviews.

Public speaking has a been a priority because when I was younger I was often told I spoke too fast and that I could benefit from adding more intonation to my presentations. With that feedback in mind, I took locution lessons and joined public speaking clubs. Generally, you improve on the skill you practice. I had practiced speaking in a safe, controlled environment using rehearsed responses. I therefore, improved on speaking in a safe, controlled environment using rehearsed responses. That is NOT the public speaking skill you will need for data science interviews. Here, you need to practice not knowing, being correct, and STAYING CHILL. I did not practice not knowing, which is bound to happen at a technical test and carrying on. I let a couple of questions or situations, which unsettled me, have an impact on the rest of my interview. I let a tiny portion of my interview that wasn‚Äôt great bleed into the rest. Not ideal.

What would I do now?

I love public speaking clubs like Toastmasters because they have made me aware of my impact on an audience. I can tell when people are zoning out and what I have done to cause that. It‚Äôs helped me improve my pace, pauses and intonation. But what I think would be better practice in this context is joining a debate club. In a debate there is another team actively pushing against you and trying, to some extent, to undermine what you‚Äôre saying. Constantly. If they‚Äôre skilled, they might even attempt to provoke a reaction. That‚Äôs what I need to practice. Can anyone recommend debate clubs in London?

5 What would I do again?

5.1 Be yourself (or as close as possible)

Adopting some sort of persona and putting a mask during an interview is not a good idea. On top of the technical work you‚Äôll be doing each day, you‚Äôll have to do the emotional work of transforming your personality. This is exhausting. Instead, just be yourself. However, if during the interview I project myself as someone I‚Äôm not and they make an offer, I‚Äôll have to transform myself each day before work.
